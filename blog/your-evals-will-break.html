Your Evals Will Break and You Won't See It Coming

May 17, 2026

We've gotten pretty good at evaluating LLMs. Benchmarks for reasoning, coding, math. Safety evals for jailbreaks and toxicity. Red-teaming. Scaling laws that predict loss before training finishes. The toolkit is solid and getting better.

Here's what I worry about: none of this is guaranteed to work at the next scale.

Not because the evals are bad. They're good. The problem is that they're all designed for the kind of model we have right now. And we have no way to know whether these evaluations will still mean anything when the system undergoes a qualitative shift — when it doesn't just get better at what it already does, but becomes a fundamentally different kind of thing.

In physics, this is called a phase transition. I think it's the most important unsolved problem in how we understand LLMs.

LLMs Are Thermodynamic (and This Matters)

Here's a fact that deserves more attention: when you train LLMs with different random seeds, different data, different hyperparameters, models at the same scale converge to similar capabilities. Same abilities emerge at roughly the same compute thresholds. GPT-4-class models from different labs, trained independently, end up strikingly similar.

This is what thermodynamic systems look like. A gas has $10^{23}$ molecules flying around unpredictably, but the macro-level stuff — temperature, pressure, entropy — is rock solid. You can't predict any single molecule, but you can predict the aggregate with extraordinary precision.

LLMs are the same. Any individual output is noisy and unpredictable. But capabilities, behavioral distributions, alignment profiles — those are macroscopic quantities, and they follow lawful patterns. Scaling laws are basically equations of state: compute in, predictable behavior out, the way $PV = nRT$ connects pressure, volume, and temperature.

This is why our evals work. Benchmarks measure the macro state. They're reliable because the macro-level regularities are real.

But here's the thing about thermodynamic systems: they have a failure mode that has burned every field that forgot about it.

Phase Transitions Break Everything

Water doesn't gradually become steam. It jumps. At a critical point, the equation of state changes character. The fundamental variables — temperature, pressure, volume — remain the same, but the relationships between them transform discontinuously. The liquid's equation of state simply does not apply to the gas. Every regularity you carefully documented in one phase can mislead you about the other.

This isn't a measurement failure. The quantities you're measuring are still well-defined. But the patterns you learned from them — the correlations, the trends, the rules of thumb — no longer hold.

We may have already seen hints of this with LLMs, though the evidence is more contested than it first appears.

In-context learning, chain-of-thought reasoning, tool use — these capabilities seemed to appear suddenly at certain scales, looking like textbook phase transitions. But there's an important counterpoint: Schaeffer et al. (2023) showed that many of these apparent "jumps" are artifacts of discontinuous metrics like exact-match accuracy. Switch to a continuous metric and the capability often scales smoothly, with no transition in sight.

This is a genuinely important finding, but I don't think it settles the question — and in a way, it makes my point sharper. If we can't even tell whether a past transition was a real qualitative shift or a metric artifact, what does that say about our ability to detect the next one? We're not just blind to future transitions. We can't even reliably characterize the ones that already happened. Our instruments are so poorly calibrated for this question that we're still debating whether the data shows a phase transition or a smooth curve.

Either way — whether capabilities emerge smoothly or in jumps — the evaluation problem remains. If capabilities scale smoothly but our benchmarks use discontinuous metrics, then our evals are giving us a misleading picture of the system. And if genuine phase transitions do occur, we have no way to predict them. Both scenarios end in the same place: our evaluation infrastructure can surprise us.

We're Measuring the Current Phase

Here's the problem stated precisely: to predict a phase transition, you need to know the order parameter — the macroscopic quantity that changes discontinuously at the critical point. For a magnet, it's magnetization. For liquid-gas, it's density. The order parameter tells you what to watch and how close you are to the boundary.

For LLMs, we don't have order parameters. Not for capability transitions. Not for alignment transitions. Not for anything.

Every benchmark we use — MMLU, HumanEval, GSM8K — measures what models can do now. They're useful within a regime and completely silent about what comes next. When a new capability emerges that no benchmark tests for, we scramble to build an evaluation after the fact. We saw this when reasoning benchmarks saturated overnight after chain-of-thought appeared. We saw it when coding benchmarks became trivial once models could use tools. We'll see it again.

This means our entire evaluation infrastructure is structurally reactive. We measure the system after it has changed. We never predict the change.

Safety evaluation has the same problem, maybe worse. Every safety team monitors refusal rates, jailbreak success rates, hallucination frequencies, toxicity scores. These all describe the safety profile of the current phase. If the system undergoes a transition — if a new capability enables failure modes that existing classifiers weren't trained to detect — these metrics become as meaningless as measuring water's viscosity to predict steam's behavior. You'd be monitoring the wrong thing and wouldn't know it.

Scaling Laws Won't Save You

I know the instinctive response: "but we have scaling laws; we can extrapolate."

No. This misunderstands what phase transitions are. The whole point is that the smooth curve breaks. Scaling laws are the equations of state of one phase. They hold within that phase and they break at the boundary. The ideal gas law beautifully describes steam; it tells you absolutely nothing about the liquid water on the other side of the transition, because the law simply doesn't apply there. Each phase has its own equation of state, and the transition between them is exactly where both equations fail.

Our scaling laws are basically Kepler's laws — empirically accurate, descriptively powerful, not derived from anything deeper. We can plot the curves. We can't explain the exponents. And without a first-principles theory, we can't predict where the curves will break.

Physics solved this with deeper machinery: partition functions, renormalization group theory, universality classes. We have none of this for LLMs. We don't have the formal framework that would let us predict, from architecture and training alone, where qualitative transitions occur.

We don't have Boltzmann. And without Boltzmann, we can't predict when the water will boil.

So What Do We Do

If this is as fundamental as I think it is, the field needs to invest differently. Not by throwing away current evals — they work and they matter — but by building the infrastructure to predict when they'll stop working.

Find the order parameters. This is the big one. What macroscopic quantities signal a qualitative transition — in capability, in alignment, in behavioral character? Mechanistic interpretability might be the best path here. Not for predicting individual outputs (that's tracking molecules), but for identifying the structural variables that sit at phase boundaries. If we knew what to measure, we'd know what to watch.

Build scaling laws for everything, not just loss. We need empirical scaling curves for reasoning depth, tool-use sophistication, planning horizon, robustness to distribution shift, deceptive capacity — all as functions of scale. Even without theory, watching how these evolve gives us early warning. If a metric that's been smoothly improving suddenly changes slope, pay attention.

Build evals that detect their own obsolescence. If we can't predict transitions, we can at least notice when one is happening. Monitor the meta-signals: is the distribution of benchmark scores changing character? Is the correlation structure between evaluations shifting? Is the model behaving in ways that are orthogonal to your measurement axes? These second-order signals might be the most reliable early warning we get.

Invest in first-principles theory. Our scaling laws are curve fits. We need the theory that derives them — that explains the exponents, predicts where they break, and tells us what matters at larger scales. This is the hardest item on the list and the one that would most fundamentally change our ability to see what's coming.

The Honest Picture

I want to be precise. I'm not saying a dangerous phase transition is imminent. I'm saying we can't currently tell whether one is coming, and that that is the problem.

Our evaluation methodology is good. It describes the current phase well. But it's structurally post-hoc — it tells us where we are, never where we're going. And we've already been surprised multiple times by capabilities we didn't anticipate — whether those were genuine phase transitions or smooth curves that our metrics made look like jumps, the surprise itself is the indictment.

The most urgent evaluation problem is not building better benchmarks for what models can do today. It's understanding, at a fundamental level, when our benchmarks will stop meaning what we think they mean.

We're measuring models we can't predict, using tools that have a known failure mode. The question isn't whether our evaluations will be surprised — they already have been, repeatedly, whether by genuine phase transitions or by our own metric choices misleading us. The question is whether we'll see the next surprise coming. Right now, we won't.
